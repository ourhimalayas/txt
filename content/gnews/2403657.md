**Pierre Kory:** You know, that's a good question. I haven't seen, to be honest. That's a cool question because I've thought about that myself. I've seen a lot of love and devotion in, at least in my patient population, that, I kind of wonder when that's going to be when some partner is going to say, you're not who I married, I'm not happy, and leave them in the illness, which is what you're not supposed to do. And, you know, in the marriage vows. But I haven't seen that very much. But clearly their relationships are affected. The partner also suffers. The partner can't go out to dinner with them. They can't go see a ballgame. They can't go on the trips they used to go on. And so they're all leading different lives. But I've seen a lot of partner support and commitment.

**Tucker Carlson:** I mean, if you knew that your life had been completely destroyed, you couldn't work. You couldn't even go out to dinner because you were forced to take a vaccine. And no one ever apologized or, you know, stood up to help you. How angry would you be?

**Pierre Kory:** The amount of anger, is. It's incalculable. The patients that I see, they are equal parts angry. I think wrongly, they feel ashamed because they were duped. You know, they did something. Many of them were actually kind of reluctant, believe that those are the worst. They really didn't want to. They pushed back a little, tried not to, and then finally succumb because their livelihood was on the line. And then they got injured. And those are the ones who kick themselves. So there's a lot of regret, shame, anger, and then some of them, there's also a lot of, you know, they're injured, they're sick, but they're active in vaccine injury groups. They try to share their experiences with things that have helped them. They advocate. They reach out to their politicians trying to bring this more to common knowledge. And, and so.

**Tucker Carlson:** But they have no power, there's nothing they can do. Right? They can't sue Pfizer.

**Pierre Kory:** Nope. Not yet.
